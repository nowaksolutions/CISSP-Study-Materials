## 🔐 Social Engineering Attacks and Defense Mechanisms

---

### 📘 Concept

Social engineering exploits **human psychology**, not technical vulnerabilities.

Attackers manipulate **trust, authority, urgency, fear, or curiosity** to bypass controls and gain access to information or systems.

---

### 🧩 Common Attack Types

**Pretexting**  
Attacker creates a **false identity or scenario** to gain trust and extract information.

**Quid Pro Quo**  
Attacker offers **something of value** in exchange for information or access.

**Spear Phishing**  
**Targeted phishing** customized for a specific individual or organization.

**Whaling**  
Highly targeted phishing focused on **executives or high-value individuals**.

**Vishing**  
Phishing conducted via **voice calls (phone/VoIP)**.

**Smishing**  
Phishing delivered through **SMS or text messages**.

**SPIM (Spam over Instant Messaging)**  
Malicious messages sent via **chat or messaging platforms**.

**SPIT (Spam over Internet Telephony)**  
Spam or phishing conducted over **VoIP systems**.

**Shoulder Surfing**  
Observing a user’s **screen or input** to capture sensitive information.

**Dumpster Diving**  
Retrieving sensitive data from **discarded materials**.

**Tailgating**  
Unauthorized individual **follows an authorized person** into a restricted area without permission.

**Piggybacking**  
Similar to tailgating, but the **authorized user knowingly allows access**.

---

### ⚠️ Attack Characteristics

Most social engineering attacks rely on:

- **Urgency** – “Act now or consequences occur”  
- **Authority** – Impersonating leadership or trusted entities  
- **Trust/Familiarity** – Appearing legitimate or known  
- **Fear or Pressure** – Forcing quick, unverified decisions  

<p align="center"><strong>Human Weakness → Control Bypass</strong></p>

---

### 🛡️ Defensive Measures

Effective defense is **people-focused**, not purely technical:

- **Continuous security awareness training**  
- **Identity verification procedures (challenge and response)**  
- **Strong security policies and enforcement**  
- **Physical controls (badges, access restrictions, escorts)**  

---

### 🎯 Why This Matters (CISSP Context)

Spans **Security and Risk Management (Domain 1)** and **Security Operations (Domain 7)**.

Social engineering represents a **high-probability, low-cost attack vector** that often bypasses technical controls entirely.

Without proper controls:

- Sensitive information is exposed  
- Unauthorized access is granted  
- Incidents occur without system-level compromise  

CISSP questions will often expect you to choose **awareness, training, or procedural controls over technical solutions**.

---

### 🧠 CISSP Decision Lens

When evaluating a scenario:

1. Identify the **human vulnerability being exploited**  
2. Determine the **type of social engineering attack**  
3. Apply **preventive controls focused on people and process**  

Default mindset:

**Reduce human susceptibility before adding technical controls**

---

### 🚨 Exam Trap

Focusing only on **technical defenses** when the root cause is **human behavior or lack of awareness**.

---

### ✅ Exam Takeaway

**Social engineering targets people, not systems.**

Primary defenses:

- **Awareness training**  
- **Verification procedures**  
- **Policy enforcement**
